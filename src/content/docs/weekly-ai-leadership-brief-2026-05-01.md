## What changed this week

- AI adoption is outpacing governance frameworks, creating risks in data privacy, security, and ethical use (sources 2, 7).
- Connected, high-quality data is essential for AI-driven sustainability and decision-making; trust in AI depends on data integrity (source 1).
- New leadership appointments at major banks (RBC, Lloyds) signal a focus on AI and digital transformation at the executive level (source 9).
- Healthcare leaders report that AI deployment requires iterative learning and integration with clinical workflows, not just technology (source 10).
- Engineers are widely using AI tools, often without formal organizational oversight, raising governance concerns (source 11).

## Leadership implications

- Executives must prioritize establishing robust AI governance frameworks that keep pace with adoption to mitigate risks (sources 2, 7).
- Data strategy should focus on connectivity and quality to build trust in AI outputs, especially for critical decisions (source 1).
- AI operating models need to bridge the gap between AI capabilities and existing enterprise systems; architecture decisions (e.g., SAP Databricks) are strategic (source 8).
- Leaders should foster a culture of responsible AI use among technical staff, ensuring alignment with organizational policies (source 11).
- Cross-industry insights (healthcare, finance) show that successful AI deployment requires change management and continuous learning (sources 6, 10).

## Decisions for the next 7 days

- Review your organization's AI governance framework and identify gaps in data privacy, security, and ethical guidelines (sources 2, 5).
- Assess the quality and connectivity of your data assets to ensure they are AI-ready for decision-making (source 1).
- Evaluate your enterprise architecture (e.g., data lakehouse vs. traditional systems) to support scalable AI (source 8).
- Initiate a conversation with engineering teams about their AI tool usage and establish clear policies (source 11).
- Plan a leadership workshop on AI risk management and governance, drawing on lessons from healthcare and finance (sources 6, 10).
